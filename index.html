Concept

Selected renovation work focusing on the relationship between existing structures and new layers, exploring how aging condominium stock can gain renewed spatial and cultural value.

During Japan’s economic bubble of the 1980s, condominium construction increased dramatically. More than forty years later, the average age of condominiums circulating on the secondary market continues to rise. In 2024, the average age of newly listed existing condominiums in the Tokyo metropolitan area reached 30.22 years, up from 25.84 years in 2019—clear evidence of an aging housing stock.

Today, a 40-year-old condominium is often perceived as “old.” However, as the volume of existing condominiums continues to grow, the threshold of “old” will inevitably shift. While traditional houses in Japan are often embraced as kominka—valued for their age and character—condominiums have yet to acquire a similar cultural appreciation. This project explores the potential for renovation to expand such values by preserving and reinterpreting existing materials and elements.

Rather than adopting a complete skeleton-and-infill approach, this renovation employs an upcycling strategy. Retaining the logic of the original 4LDK layout, the plan was reconfigured into an open and flexible 2LDK. Existing partitions were partly removed, and original studs were exposed to create a lattice-like divider that gently zones the space while supporting a new counter.

A former tatami room received flooring with a different plank width and slightly darker tone, preventing the enlarged living space from becoming monotonous and adding rhythm to the interior. Indirect lighting runs along the upper perimeter of the walls, softly stitching together old and new layers.

The kitchen, once enclosed, was opened up using exposed studs as a permeable boundary. Light now penetrates deep into the plan, and added shelving between the studs provides storage, while a single grid bay consolidates intercom and switches. Inspired by the way aged detached houses celebrate their accumulated character, this renovation embraces and reactivates what already exists.

By reinterpreting the given rather than replacing it, the project proposes a renewed lifespan and value for the aging condominium as architectural stock.
