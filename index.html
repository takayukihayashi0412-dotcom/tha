Concept

Selected renovation project focusing on the relationship between existing structures and newly added layers, exploring how aging condominium stock can acquire renewed spatial and cultural value.

During Japan’s economic bubble of the 1980s, condominium construction increased dramatically. More than forty years later, the average age of condominiums circulating on the secondary market continues to rise. In 2024, the average age of newly listed existing condominiums in the Tokyo metropolitan area reached 30.22 years, up from 25.84 years in 2019—clear evidence of an aging housing stock.

Today, a 40-year-old condominium is often perceived as “old.” However, as the volume of existing condominiums continues to grow, the threshold of what is considered “old” will inevitably shift. While traditional houses in Japan are often embraced as kominka, valued for their age and accumulated character, condominiums have yet to attain a comparable cultural appreciation.

Rather than adopting a complete skeleton-and-infill approach, the renovation employs an upcycling strategy. Retaining the underlying logic of the original 4LDK layout, the plan was reconfigured into an open and flexible 2LDK.

The former tatami room was finished with flooring of a different plank width and a slightly darker tone, introducing subtle rhythm. The kitchen was opened to the living area using exposed studs as a permeable boundary.

By reinterpreting the given rather than replacing it, the project proposes a renewed lifespan and architectural value for the aging condominium as housing stock.
