Concept

Selected renovation project focusing on the relationship between existing structures and newly added layers, exploring how aging condominium stock can acquire renewed spatial and cultural value.

During Japan’s economic bubble of the 1980s, condominium construction increased dramatically. More than forty years later, the average age of condominiums circulating on the secondary market continues to rise. In 2024, the average age of newly listed existing condominiums in the Tokyo metropolitan area reached 30.22 years, up from 25.84 years in 2019—clear evidence of an aging housing stock.

Today, a 40-year-old condominium is often perceived as “old.” However, as the volume of existing condominiums continues to grow, the threshold of what is considered “old” will inevitably shift. While traditional houses in Japan are often embraced as kominka, valued for their age and accumulated character, condominiums have yet to attain a comparable cultural appreciation. This project explores the potential of renovation to expand such values by preserving and reinterpreting existing materials and architectural elements.

Rather than adopting a complete skeleton-and-infill approach, the renovation employs an upcycling strategy. Retaining the underlying logic of the original 4LDK layout, the plan was reconfigured into an open and flexible 2LDK. Portions of the existing partitions were removed, and the original studs were exposed to form a lattice-like divider that gently zones the space while supporting a new counter.

The former tatami room was finished with flooring of a different plank width and a slightly darker tone, preventing the enlarged living space from becoming monotonous and introducing a subtle rhythm to the interior. Indirect lighting runs along the upper perimeter of the walls, softly stitching together the old and new layers.

The kitchen, once enclosed, was opened to the living area, with exposed studs serving as a permeable boundary. Light now penetrates deep into the plan, while shelving inserted between the studs provides additional storage. Within a single grid bay, intercom and switches are neatly consolidated. Inspired by the way aged detached houses celebrate their accumulated layers over time, this renovation embraces and reactivates what already exists.

By reinterpreting the given rather than replacing it, the project proposes a renewed lifespan and architectural value for the aging condominium as housing stock.
