Concept

This renovation reconsiders a typical Japanese condominium that has aged over time, using the concept of “ordinary” (futsū) as its guiding framework. Rather than pursuing novelty or stylistic distinction, the project aims for versatility by carefully reinterpreting what already exists.

Here, ordinary does not mean average or neutral. It refers to a shared, almost unconscious sense that operates smoothly within everyday life—an indistinct yet reliable contour that supports actions without calling attention to itself. This ordinariness contains an inherent adaptability, remaining open to multiple uses and values without being fixed to a single interpretation.

In contemporary Japan, the number of aging condominiums on the secondary market continues to increase. Renovation has become the dominant response, yet many projects remain limited to superficial updates—new finishes, cosmetic “modernization,” or nostalgic references—without questioning the underlying spatial logic of the building stock. This project proposes an alternative: instead of replacing what is perceived as old, it identifies which elements should not be changed, and recalibrates them to function again within present-day life.

The design avoids full-scale demolition and reconstruction. Instead, it operates as an upcycling process through three architectural interventions.

First, wooden studs were exposed by removing gypsum boards. Unlike contemporary light-gauge steel framing, these studs follow traditional Japanese modules of 303 or 450 millimeters. Treated neither as walls nor as furniture but as linear elements, they create ambiguous boundaries that gently articulate spaces. This approach can be read as a contemporary interpretation of traditional Japanese spatial devices such as shoji screens or latticework.

Second, the reinforced concrete structure was returned to its raw state. Japanese RC apartment buildings are typically constructed with high precision, allowing the structural surface itself to function as a finish. By removing wallpaper and tiles and applying minimal repair, the structure is repositioned as a material unique to condominium architecture rather than something to be concealed.

Third, large beams—often considered problematic in older buildings—were reinterpreted. By inserting shallow inner eaves beneath them and integrating indirect lighting, the beams become environmental devices that modulate light and perception, rather than obstacles to be hidden.

Through these operations, the project achieves a layered spatial quality that goes beyond superficial “Japanese-style” expressions. The method also reduces construction waste to less than half that of a standard renovation. Importantly, this approach is not tied to a specific plan: it offers a transferable strategy applicable to other aging condominiums, suggesting a realistic and sustainable architectural value for Japan’s expanding housing stock.
